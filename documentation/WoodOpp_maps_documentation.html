Woodland opportunity mapping

 

Use and Applications

 

Purpose: to identify and prioritise opportunities for woodland creation across mainland Cornwall to enhance biodiversity, connectivity of existing woodland and ecosystem services.

What does the map show? The opportunity map identifies areas of mainland Cornwall that would deliver the greatest benefit as a result of woodland creation. The area identified is sub-divided into three exclusive bands allowing the identification of areas that would lead to a 50, 100 and 200% increase in the existing woodland cover of the county.

Intended uses: The map is intended to inform strategic planning to increase woodland cover in Cornwall and also to inform more general land use strategies.

Notes on the use and interpretation of the opportunity map:  It is important to recognize some key aspects of the map and the underlying methodology by which it as generated:

·      The spatial unit of prioritization is 100 square metre cells although this does not necessarily imply benefits require woodland creation across the whole of the respective cell. The mapping is not suitable for assessing benefits of small-scale plantings of under 1Ha.

·       Rankings are assigned uniquely on an estimate of potential ecosystem service and biodiversity gains under mature woodland.

·      Cell rankings are dependent upon maintaining existing woodland cover and the creation of woodland in more highly ranked cells. This inter-dependence of cell rankings is most evident by considering the connectivity of woodlands. The creation of a woodland ‘corridor’ is only meaningful if existing woodlands that it links are maintained and the whole of the corridor is created.

·      Higher rankings generally favour areas that deliver multiple benefits.

·      Rankings are assigned on the basis of current land cover and factors affecting demand and delivery of ecosystem services such as population centres.

·      The area that does not feature among the most highly ranked cells does not imply that woodland planting will deliver no service benefits or biodiversity value.

·      The type of woodland is not specified but will clearly influence the magnitude of service and biodiversity gains.

 

Methodological Approach

 

      i.         Identify existing woodland areas: these areas are designated as the last areas to be removed, ensuring that they inform the ranking of all opportunity areas.

    ii.         Identify areas to exclude a priori from woodland creation: areas not to be considered for woodland creation were identified, such as urban and quarry areas, deep peat soils, moorland, exposed coastal zone, priority habitats and existing protected areas. All remaining areas are treated as potential woodland creation areas.

   iii.         Identify ‘constraining’ factors: constraints are factors that are likely to reduce the likelihood, or increase the cost, of realising woodland benefits. Factors include topographical constraints, such as wind exposure, or the presence of other land cover or features such as existing buildings or infrastructure. All areas that include identified constraints are assigned a negative weighting that will lower their relative value during the prioritization of opportunities. Opportunity costs (the loss of land availability for other uses) have generally not been included.

   iv.         Identify ‘facilitating’ factors: that will increase the likelihood, or lower the cost, of realising the benefits of wood creation opportunities. For example, steep slopes could be considered a facilitating factor as they can restrict alternative land uses, particularly agriculture. Each factor will be given a positive weighting in the analysis.

    v.         Identify and estimate the relative value of woodland “benefits”: each benefit will be assigned a positive weighting. The calculation of benefits will generally involve an estimation of both the demand for, and the potential provision of ecosystem services from woodland creation although the methodologies for doing so vary between different types of benefit.

   vi.         Spatial prioritization of cells: was carried out using the Zonation model of spatial prioritization (Lehtomäki et al. 2016), which ranks all non-excluded cells on the basis of their weighted potential benefits, facilitating factors and constraints.

Data and weightings

 

The type of data used for the spatial prioritization of the map grid cells were:

·      Mutually exclusive presence / absence data, such as the dominant land cover type.

·      Non-exclusive presence / absence data, such as statutory designations.

·      Non-exclusive scaled quantitative data that provided an indication of the relative importance of different features (for example hedgerow lengths or catchment quality) or service provision (such as quantified estimates of ecosystem services).

The relative importance of different services, habitats and features informing the prioritization is determined by assigning weights. Positive weights describe the relative value or benefits of different services and features. Negative weights indicate a constraint or cost of realising benefits.

Cell ranking

 

The spatial prioritization works by iteratively removing the least ‘valuable’, lowest ranked cells (see figure 1) on the basis of:

·      Weighted fraction of all remaining features that values cells containing a high overall proportion of individual features (ie it will most value cells containing a high proportion of a habitat type or service).

·      An ‘added benefit function’ that sums the normalised distribution across all features and so tends to remove cells with low feature diversity before cells with higher feature diversity, such as cells providing multiple ecosystem services.

·      Aggregation of remaining areas – a ‘boundary length penalty’ seeks to reduce edge to area ratio of all remaining cells (irrespective of the type of features within those cells). In effect this will aggregate and connect together all prioritized areas rather than specific habitat types. In effect for the woodlands opportunity mapping this will tend to aggregate together existing woodland (removed last) and potential woodlands site, encouraging greater connectivity between woodland areas.

·      Existing woodland areas are designated as the last areas to be removed, ensuring that they inform the ranking of all other areas.

Key data types and weightings

 

The identification of, and attribution of weights to, benefits, constraints and facilitating factors has drawn on the methods and expertise applied in previous opportunity mapping such as work by Forests Research, Woodland trust and other researchers.

Estimates of ecosystem services provided by woodland correspond to estimates of the potential gain in service provision under mature woodland. Such estimates are subject to greater uncertainty than estimates of current service provision and to reflect this uncertainty lower weightings were given to service provision layers.

Unlike some previous studies, no attempt has been made to discriminate between different kinds of woodland. In terms of flood mitigation benefits, for example, discrimination is often made between riparian (riverside), floodplain and catchment woodlands (for example Woodlands for Wetlands REF). No such discrimination is made here as the mitigation effects (and associated uncertainties) of these different kinds of woodlands are captured in the calculation of potential flood mitigation benefits.

Note on excluded areas

 

To avoid areas of potential heathland restoration to be considered as woodland opportunities we have adopted the Moorline areas as excluded from consideration. . Sand dune type soils have also been considered a constraint on potential woodland opportunities.

 Forests planted on mineral soils are generally accepted to result in increased soil C content. Carbon sequestration has often been found to be highest on arable soils previously having very low soil Carbon content. In some circumstances, however, afforestation on organo-mineral (e.g. peaty gleys, peaty podzols) or organic (e.g. deep peats) soils may result in substantial loss of soil C due to soil disturbance during forest planting. Evidence suggests that afforestation can change deep peat from a carbon sink to a carbon source, thus contributing to global warming. As a result, the Forestry Commission has concluded that for conservation and wider environmental reasons there should be a strong presumption against further forestry expansion on raised bog and extensive blanket bog (P&A). Given the high biodiversity value and declining abundance of peatland habitats, we have adopted peaty soils as a major constraint on woodland opportunities
